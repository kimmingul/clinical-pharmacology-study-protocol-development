I. INTRODUCTION  16
 17
This guidance is intended to assist applicants of human prescription drug and biological 18
products2 in determining the appropriate placement and content of drug interaction (DI) 19
information in labeling as described in the regulations for the content and format of labeling for 20
human prescription drug and biological products.3,4 The purpose of this guidance is to provide 21
recommendations to help ensure that appropriate DI information is consistently placed in the 22
proper sections and subsections within labeling so that the information is clear and accessible to 23
health care practitioners (HCPs) and includes content that guides the safe and effective use of the 24
drug. Applicants should follow the recommendations in this guidance when developing this 25
section of labeling for a new drug submitted to the FDA under a new drug application under 26
section 505(b) of the FD&C Act or a biologics license application under section 351(a) of the 27

1. Instructions for Preventing or Managing DIs 120
 121
Instructions to prevent or manage clinically significant DIs in the DRUG INTERACTIONS 122
section must be accurate,23 specific, and practical24 and should be actionable for the HCP. For 123
example, a prevention or management recommendation for a clinically significant DI of the 124
subject drug with warfarin that describes a specific change in the frequency or timing of  125
international normalized ratio (INR) monitoring is more informative for HCPs than a non-126
specific recommendation such as “monitor INR” that reflects standard clinical practice for 127
patients taking warfarin. Applicants should use specific statements rather than vague or 128
ambiguous statements (e.g., “avoid concomitant use” is preferred over “use with caution,” 129
“reduce dosage” is preferred over “adjust dosage”) and use active voice instead of passive voice.   130

2. Mechanism of the Clinically Significant DI 213
 214
The mechanism of the clinically significant DI, if known, must be briefly described in the DRUG 215
INTERACTIONS section.28 A clinically significant DI can be the result of pharmacokinetic 216
(PK) and/or pharmacodynamic (PD) changes.   217
 218
• For PK metabolism- (e.g., cytochrome P450) and transporter-based interactions, the 219
subject drug should be identified as an inhibitor, inducer, or substrate. Additionally, the 220
change in drug exposure29 should be briefly summarized (e.g., increases drugozide-x 221
exposure, decreases drugozide-x exposure), with a cross-reference to the supporting DI 222
information in the Pharmacokinetics subsection in the CLINICAL PHARMACOLOGY 223
section. For example, the DRUG INTERACTIONS section would state:   224
 225
“Drugozide-x is a CYP3A substrate. Strong CYP3A inhibitors increase
226
drugozide-x exposure [see Clinical Pharmacology (12.3)].”
227
 228
• For PD-based interactions, the nature of the PD effects should be described. For example, 229
the DRUG INTERACTIONS section would state:  230
 231
“Both drugozide-x and non-selective MAO inhibitors inhibit catecholamine
232
metabolism.”

4. Drug Interacting Classes and Examples of Drugs Within Drug Interacting 264
Classes   265
 266
When there are clinically significant DIs of the subject drug with all drugs within a drug 267
interacting class,30 the FDA recommends that applicants identify only the drug interacting class 268
and avoid naming the drugs within the drug interacting class in the DRUG INTERACTIONS 269
section.31 In this situation, reasons that the FDA generally recommends not naming examples of 270
drugs within the drug interacting class in this section include the following: 271
 272
• If examples of individual drugs within a drug interacting class are included in labeling, 273
those examples may become less relevant over time (e.g., new drugs in the drug 274
interacting class may be approved, or selected examples of drugs in the drug interacting 275
class may no longer be commonly used in clinical practice or may no longer be available 276
because they have been withdrawn from the market).  277
